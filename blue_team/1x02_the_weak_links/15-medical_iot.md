# The Medical IoT

## BD Alaris Assessment

**Researching the actual bulletin turned up something the scan report itself gets wrong** — worth documenting carefully rather than just repeating Finding 010's claim.

**What the vulnerability actually is**: CISA advisory **ICSMA-20-317-01** (CVE-2020-25165) describes a network session authentication weakness between the BD Alaris PC Unit (Model 8015) and the BD Alaris Systems Manager. An attacker on the network can modify configuration headers in transit, enabling a denial-of-service against the pump. CVSS v3 base score is **6.5** (`AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:N/I:L/A:L`) — notably *lower* than the 7.5 the scan report cites, and Confidentiality-impact is `N` (none) per the vendor's own scoring, not the broader impact the finding implies.

**What BD actually recommends, and the version discrepancy**: This is the important part. CISA's advisory states the affected versions are **PC Unit Model 8015, version 9.33.1 and earlier**, and **Systems Manager version 4.33 and earlier**. The fix shipped in **PC Unit software 12.1.1 and newer**, and **Systems Manager 12.0.1, 12.0.2, 12.1.0, and 12.1.2**. Finding 010 reports the scanned pumps are running **firmware 12.1.2** and describes that as the *vulnerable* version — but per BD's own advisory, **12.1.2 is one of the fixed versions**, not an affected one.

**Has MedDefense implemented the recommendation?** Based on the version number alone, the honest answer looks like **yes, probably** — 12.1.2 is past the fix line BD published. This looks like a real discrepancy in Finding 010's CVE attribution, not a confirmed vulnerability, and it's worth flagging back to SecurePoint for the same reason Task 11 flagged three other findings: a scanner (or a report author transcribing its output) matching a version number against a CVE database is exactly the kind of check that produces false positives when BD's PCU firmware and Systems Manager use overlapping-but-distinct version schemes. **This should be validated directly against BD's Cybersecurity Trust Center bulletin for this exact CVE before anyone spends remediation effort "upgrading" a device that may already be patched.**

**What's still real and doesn't depend on that discrepancy**: The other half of Finding 010 — **7 of 7 scanned pumps still have unchanged default web-interface credentials (`admin`/`admin`)** — is a completely separate, independently-verified issue. Detecting a working default login doesn't depend on version-matching or CVE databases at all; either the scanner logged in with `admin`/`admin` or it didn't. This part of the finding is real regardless of whether the CVE attribution is accurate, and it's arguably the more dangerous half: it requires no exploit development, no firmware research, and no CVE at all — just trying the one credential pair every BD Alaris ships with by default.


## Philips IntelliVue Assessment

**What data flows through these interfaces**: The web management interface exposes device configuration and, typically, live vital-sign displays (heart rate, blood pressure, SpO2, ECG waveform, respiration, temperature). Port 2575 is the HL7 (Health Level 7) interface — per Finding 016, explicitly used for "patient data exchange." HL7 messages of this kind (ORU observation-result messages, in HL7's own terminology) don't just carry raw numbers; they're typically tagged with the patient identifiers needed to route the data to the right chart — name, MRN, date of birth, alongside the vitals themselves. That combination is PHI by definition, not just clinical telemetry.

**What an attacker with network access could see or do**: With zero authentication beyond network reachability, an attacker anywhere on the flat network could passively intercept HL7 traffic to harvest real-time PHI tied to specific patients — a direct confidentiality breach. More seriously, the exposed web management interface plausibly allows reconfiguration of the monitor itself: alarm thresholds, displayed values, or device settings, depending on what the interface exposes. Falsifying or suppressing an alarm on a monitor that's supposed to alert staff to a crashing patient isn't a data-confidentiality problem at all — it's a direct integrity/availability attack on the one system standing between a deteriorating patient and a clinician noticing in time.


## The Patient Safety Dimension

A compromised IT workstation is a confidentiality and integrity problem for *data* — records get stolen, files get ransomed, systems go down — all serious, all costly, and all ultimately recoverable from backups or breach-notification processes. A compromised infusion pump or patient monitor is a confidentiality *and* integrity problem for a person's physical body, in real time, with no undo. A falsified dosage command on an infusion pump can deliver a lethal overdose or a fatal underdose of a medication like insulin or a vasopressor within minutes — there's no backup copy of a patient's bloodstream to restore from. A suppressed or falsified alarm on a monitor can let a genuine cardiac or respiratory event go unnoticed by exactly the clinical staff whose job is to catch it, causing a preventable death purely through omission. The worst case for a compromised infusion pump is a specific patient's death, potentially at scale if an attacker targets multiple pumps simultaneously — a kind of extortion scenario ("pay us or dosages change hospital-wide") that has no IT-system equivalent. The worst case for a compromised workstation, by contrast, is financial loss, regulatory penalty, and reputational damage — severe, but not the same category of harm.


## The Remediation Challenge: Why Medical Devices Are Harder to Patch

**1. Regulatory (FDA clearance).** Infusion pumps, patient monitors, and imaging devices are FDA-cleared as a specific, validated hardware-plus-software configuration. A hospital can't unilaterally change the underlying OS or apply an arbitrary firmware update the way it patches a Windows workstation — doing so outside vendor-sanctioned channels can jeopardize the device's regulatory clearance and the hospital's own liability position. Patches essentially have to come from, or be explicitly blessed by, the manufacturer.

**2. Vendor dependency.** Only BD, Siemens, or Philips can produce and certify a validated update for their respective devices — MedDefense's own IT/security team has no ability to independently patch the underlying software the way they can for a general-purpose Linux or Windows server. If the vendor doesn't ship a fix (or, as with the fully EOL Windows XP-based MRI controller, never will again), there is no patch available at any price short of full device replacement.

**3. Operational/uptime constraints.** These devices generally can't be taken offline the way a file server tolerates a scheduled reboot. Patching an infusion pump or an MRI controller means coordinating with clinical staff, potentially rescheduling patient procedures, and often involves biomedical/clinical engineering rather than a remote IT push — there's no equivalent of patching outside business hours when the "business hours" in question are a hospital that operates around the clock.

**4. Validation burden (bonus factor).** Even a vendor-approved patch typically needs to be validated by the hospital's own biomedical engineering team before deployment, since these devices interact directly with patient physiology. A broken patch on a workstation is an inconvenience; a broken patch on an infusion pump is a patient-safety incident in its own right — which makes hospitals appropriately slow and cautious about deploying updates even once they exist, unlike the "patch Tuesday" cadence expected of ordinary IT systems.
